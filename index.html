Lecture Notes:

What is a myth? In Greek, mythos refers to ‘word, saying, tale, story, narrative, fiction, legend, fable, plot’ — a variety of meanings!

In the context of our course, what is a myth (specifically, what is it from that list, and what is it not)? Another Greek word, philomythos, literally means “lover of myth.”

Aristotle uses the word philomythos in an introduction to his “Metaphysics.” He’s describing the origin of philosophy; humans felt that they were ignorant and wanted explorations for the wonders they saw. In Aristotle’s words, the philosophos (lover of philosophy) is like the philomythos in that both enjoy dealing with wonders.

Emperor Julian the Apostate, who wanted to reverse the Christianization of the Roman Empire, also uses the word philomythos to describe why he wanted a reversion to paganism. According to Julian, the Christians tell a fiction about Jesus as God that he cannot bring himself to believe. He argues that the reason so many people are falling for Christianity is because it appeals to the part of the soul that loves myth — e.g. the part of the soul that loves a fictional, made-up story.

Two keys to the Greek conception of myths: on one level, they are fundamentally serious, as Aristotle likens them to philosophy, on another level, they are definitely fictional, as Julian describes them.

On another level, a myth is a traditional story. It follows normal story structure — climax, resolution, etc. — and it’s also a story that is passed down through generations (hence “tradition”).

We can compare myths to folk tales, e.g. Cinderella. Folk tales are also traditional stories, but they are not myths — why? There are a lot of folk tale motifs in mythology, but folk tales are not as profound or divine as mythological stories. Folk tales are typically told for pleasure, and they express the aspirations of the common people.

We can also compare myths to legends or sagas, e.g. George Washington and the cherry tree. A legend is a traditional folk tale that is conspicuously anchored in history. Sometimes, this overlaps with mythology; for example, the Trojan War has “legends” anchored in history surrounding it.

So, a myth is a traditional story, but NOT all traditional stories are myths.

For what reasons do myths originate?

-   Reflections of the human psyche

-   Records of a custom or an institution

-   Explanations of a natural phenomenon

-   Memorializations of religious rites or cults

-   Explanations of difficult human questions

Freud argued that a myth is like a dream in the sense that both express wishes for something that cannot be fulfilled in real life.

Jung argued that a myth expresses what he calls the “collective unconscious,” addressing problems or phenomena that we collectively have or experience.

Lévi-Strauss’s theory of “structuralism” argues that the human mind operates in polarities aka dichotomies (raw/cooked, yes/no, wild/tamed) and myths mediate between the polarities.

Myths are also used to cement institutions — for example, Apollo as the “god of foundation” guiding city-building from the oracle at Delphi.

Myths can be used to cement natural phenomena — for example, a story of the battle between the gods and the giants (Athena vs. Enceladus). The story ends with Enceladus being imprisoned below Mt. Etna, which explains the volcanic activity beneath the mountain.

Myths can also just be sacred tales; this is perhaps the most common reason of all.

Most of the myths we will discuss in class are quite multifaceted and diverse, so they lend themselves to different angles of approach. Myths were a sort of vocabulary that were used to address and talk about different issues. For example, Heracles is a heroic figure who conquers enemies and a great emblem of what we can aspire to be in terms of physical strength and the civilizing spirit of humanity, or Heracles is a god whose persona can be adopted by various philosophies (e.g. he becomes an emblem of philosophical choice in Heracles at the Crossroads), or Heracles is someone with fantastic, excessive appetites (if he’s thirsty, he drinks the whole river) who is used as a figure of comedy.

We’re also going to be talking about some examples of reception of myth in modern and contemporary culture — for example, Wonder Woman (aka Diana, Greek name).

Lecture Notes:

Theogony = the birth (gony) of the gods (theo)

Cosmogony = the birth (gony) of the cosmos (cosmo)

Effectively, the Greeks believed that the birth of the gods WAS also the birth of the cosmos, because the universe was infused with divinity.

We use Hesiod, an archaic poet, for his interpretations of these myths — but he did NOT invent them. Sources:

1.The epic tradition, which Hesiod shared with Homer. The names of the gods and what they do come from this.

2.The Greek popular tradition. Some other ideas come from this — for example, the concept of an earth mother (Gaia) being the source of life.

3.Myths imported from the Near East, specifically from the Hittites. They specifically came from Anatolia (present-day Turkey), most notably from the myth The Kingdom of Heaven. The idea of succession and multiple generations of divinities comes from these myths.

Hesiod doesn’t invent these stories; instead, he reorganizes them. Essentially, what he’s writing is a genealogical and didactic poem. It is designed to a) provide an index of all the gods and b) to provide a history of how the gods came to be. This is not an encyclopedia; it’s an organized picture of how the gods and the universe came into being.

So, how does Hesiod develop this?

1.Progressive differentiation: from greater gods come smaller gods. For example, from the ocean god Pontos (meaning “sea”) comes other water gods of rivers, etc.

2.He moves forward from the physical universe to deified aspects of humanity — from nature to human.

3.He moves from a world of dominant female divinities to a world of dominant male divinities: Gaia, the earth mother to Zeus, the king and father of gods and men.

4.The divine order changes; there is a succession of generations. We go from Ouranos to Kronos (Ouranos’s youngest son) to Zeus (Kronos’s youngest son).

Today’s lecture is about three parts: cosmogony, the reign of Ouranos, and the reign of Kronos.

PART I: COSMOGONY

At the beginning, there are four divinities:

Chaos, Gaia, Tartarus (meaning “hell”), and Eros (meaning “love”). Only two of these people produce more divinities: Chaos and Gaia.

Chaos produces darkness and night, who produce death and woe… etc. What’s important is that nothing positive comes from Chaos or her progeny! Kind of a dead end.

Gaia, however, produces more solid and tangible children. She is the major procreator and source of what comes next. Gaia produces three sorts of children:

1.Children from herself via a virgin birth — Ouranos (sky) and Pontos (sea).

2.Mating with her children with Pontos (giving birth to a whole bunch of sea powers)

3.Mating with her child Ouranos (giving birth to THREE sets of children): the cyclopes, who produce thunder and lightning, the Hecatoncheires (hundred-handed ones), and most importantly, the TITANS: our first full-scale generation of the gods.

The titans are shadowy figures without a lot of mythology in terms of organized stories about them. Their names are associated with nature. Some titans: Phoebe, Hyperion, etc. Progressive differentiation continues — for example, when Hyperion and Thea (sun and air) mate, they make gods who relate to the sky, such as Helios.

The most important titan for our purposes is Kronos, who mates with earth titan Rhea. They give birth to the next generation of gods: the OLYMPIANS.

PART II: THE REIGN OF OURANOS

The children of Ouranos are said to have hated their father from the day they were born. He put his children back in Gaia’s womb once they were born. Gaia gave a sickle to her children and told them to kill their father. Kronos waits in hiding and chops off Ouranos’s dick with a sickle when he comes to lie with Gaia.

Note a few things about this: first, Gaia is the “active figure” doing things here. Second, creation and order comes from birth in the world of myth; this comes in contrast to the Judeo-Christian canon. Anthropomorphic GODS are created. Ouranos is frustrating this process of creation (he wants to have sex with Gaia but he doesn’t want to have kids).

How do scholars read this story? They view it as a story about nature. The castration of Ouranos is seen as the differentiation between sky from earth, which is seen as necessary for the progression of the world. The second theory is that there is a religious background to this: this reflected Greek religious tradition.

In Greece there was an agricultural festival called the Kronia; thus, Kronos uses a sickle (agricultural implement) to castrate his father. Another religious way to look at the story: the prehistoric time of Greece (around 1900-1600 BC). This was a time when the people of Northern Europe came into Greece, bearing a new religion of the sky god as the prominent divinity. However, the indigenous Greeks often had religions based on an earth mother.

A third theory takes a more psychological approach to the story, proposed by Sigmund Freud. Freud saw myths as dreams, and both as some sort of wish-fulfillment. He called myths “the dreams of early humanity.” In this case, the dream is the wish of the primal horde: that the younger group (sons) would always wish to rebel against the older group (fathers). There’s a kind of anxiety in a father about replacement by the son. Thus, fathers try to stop their sons from doing this.

For Hesiod, the most important thing is to continue the process of creation, of cosmogony, and of theogony.

PART III: THE REIGN OF KRONOS

Kronos reigns with his wife Rhea. This culminates in a domestic quarrel that then leads to the birth of Zeus and the reign of ANOTHER new crew of gods.

As children are born from Rhea, Kronos swallows them into his stomach to prevent them from overthrowing them. However, Gaia develops a stratagem: when the youngest child is born, Rhea and Gaia will take him and hide him away, and then give Kronos a stone wrapped in swaddling-clothes to swallow instead. Then, all the other children will be regurgitated.

Here, Rhea (aided by her parents) tricks Kronos, who is overcome by his son Zeus (the youngest and the strongest). Zeus has many folk-tale motifs; here, we see a few.

The father is prophesized to be overcome by his son (this is seen in Oedipus).

The myth of the ogre — a monstrous figure who can only be overcome via tricks, not force.

Lecture Notes:

Three major Zeus-related obstacles to discuss: Prometheus, Titans, Typhoeus.  

First, Prometheus: he and his brother Atlas are punished by the gods. Atlas is punished to have to hold up the sky for all time; Prometheus is chained to a pillar and has his liver eaten and regenerated every day.

Next lecture, we will talk in depth about why Prometheus is punished; basically, he’s a trickster who keeps trying to fool Zeus.

Second, Titanomachy:the battle against the titans. The titans are all rebelling against the gods in a battle of epic proportions. Zeus fights back with powerful thunderbolts. Note on Zeus from the war: he is not just a force of might/power, but also a man who succeeds via political deals and alliances (e.g., his collaboration with the cyclopes, who made thunder and lightning for Zeus, and his alliance with the Hecatoncheires, who help him win the war and are in turn made the jailors of Tartarus, and his work with the goddess Styx to enlist the help of her sons, and his deal with Hecate).

A footnote to this: sometimes, this myth is conflated with the myth of gigantomachy — the battle of the Olympian gods against the giants. The giants were a race of creatures born from the earth (Gaia).

Third, Zeus’s final enemy to conquer, Typhoeus (Typhon): Typhoeus is born from Gaia. Goaded by Aphrodite, Gaia made love with Tartarus, and Typhoeus was their child. Zeus fights Typhoeus in single combat — the greatest creature of the earth vs. the man who will be king of the gods. Zeus throws Typhoeus into Tartarus, but from him come violent and damp winds (typhoons).

Zeus is not just a god of might, but also a god of intellect, dating back to his duping of Kronos with the help of Rhea. Zeus is described as conquering Kronos with his “might and craft” — he wins not just with violence but also with his mind.

Zeus is in effect elected to his supreme charge of Olympus, indicating that there will be a just cosmos going forward. Another version of this story (not Hesiod’s) says that Zeus, Poseidon, and Hades drew lots for where they would reign: sky, sea, or underworld.

Next, we move to the Seven Marriages of Zeus. Zeus is known for his promiscuity in mythology, both with divinities and with mortals. In Hesiod’s version of the story, Zeus actually marries seven times, which is expressed positively.

1.Zeus’s first wife is Metis (wisdom), wiser than all gods and men. When she was about to bear Athena, Zeus tricked her and swallowed her whole to prevent the potential threat of a child. However, Zeus then gives birth himself to Athena out of his head.

2.Zeus’s second wife is Themis (law and order, prophecy, fertility), who gives birth to two sets of children: the Horai, the seasons, and the Moirai, the fates.

3.Zeus’s third wife is Eurynome, who gives birth to the Graces (charities), who are always among the gods at parties.

4.Zeus’s fourth wife is Demeter, who is the goddess of grain. She’s one of the Olympian divinities: Zeus’s sister. They give birth to Persephone (Proserpina).

5.Zeus’s fifth wife is Mnemosyne (memory) who gives birth to the nine muses.

6.Zeus’s sixth wife is Leto, who gives birth to Apollo and Artemis.

7.Zeus’s seventh and final wife is Hera, who gives birth to three children: Hebe (youth), the cupbearer of the gods, Eileithyia (childbirth), and Ares (war). Finally, at the very end of this sequence of marriages, there is a child born exclusively from Hera: she’s mad that Zeus gave birth to Athena without usual intercourse, so she tries to one-up him and gives birth to Hephaestus (fire, crafts).

Lecture Notes:

Quiz based on reading for section: “Prometheus Bound” (Aeschylus)

Prometheus is punished because of his rebelliousness (eagle eats out his liver every day and then it grows back)

In all versions of the myth of Prometheus, there is one constant: he steals fire from the gods.

In Hesiod: Prometheus tries to trick Zeus and the gods by fake-sacrificing an ox. He tries to hide the “good part” of the ox for the humans, and feed the “bad part” of the ox to the gods.

The punishment for stealing fire in Hesiod is the Pandora’s box story.

Hesiod also tells the story of the five ages — gold, silver, bronze, demigods, and iron. This myth is designed to explain why modern life is so hard: things have only gotten worse throughout the ages, thanks to the decision making of the gods. This is the product of the deterioration of humanity.

Another story told as an example of why things are so terrible for humanity: the same myth as in Theogony, BUT with a different emphasis. Hesiod writes that man could complete a year’s work in a single day, but Prometheus tried to scam Zeus, which pissed him off.

The same story is told twice, but with essentially two different accents: in Theogony, the emphasis is placed on the punishment and the tale specifically of Prometheus; in Works and Days, the emphasis is on the reasons for human suffering. 

Another version of the myth comes from Plato and Protagoras. Protagoras is a sophist; the two thinkers are very concerned with the question of the development of humanity. 

In Protagoras, Plato tells a story to illustrate where human virtue comes from. Prometheus and Epimetheus are tasked by the gods to distribute qualities equally among all the animals as to allow them all to survive. Epimetheus does this: birds get to fly, etc. and every animal is allowed to be prevented from going extinct. However, Epimetheus (who was a little foolish) forgot that he had distributed among animals all of his qualities, so he didn’t have any qualities left to give to mankind. 

When Prometheus came to inspect the work Epimetheus had done, he realized “oh shit” so he stole fire and the “mechanical arts” (technology) and gave it to mankind. He stole from Athena and Hephaestus. However, humans were lacking one very important thing: the ability to live in a society (humanity’s Joker moment). Zeus gives all of the humans equal political wisdom. Then, Prometheus is punished for Epimetheus’s blunder. 

Key differences here: Prometheus is NOT a rebel. He’s simply following the orders of the gods. Also, he gives fire AND technology to humankind (the two being intimately connected to one another); they’re stolen not out of hostility towards Zeus but out of service to humankind. 

aetiology: the origin of some practice. Prometheus is used to explain the origin of sacrifice. There is also an argument that the Prometheus myth is an aetiology for how humans discovered fire. Another argument suggests that the story is designed to explain natural phenomena: the myth of Prometheus explains earthquakes.

The most bizarre interpretation of Prometheus is certainly that of Freud, which he writes in The Acquisition of Fire. Freud views myths as like dreams: capable of contradicting reality by expressing the wishes of an individual or a society. 

Freud views the Prometheus myth as an expression of man’s desire to urinate on the fire. He believes in symbolic representation. He believes that in myth, things can be turned into their opposites. Freud makes three key points in his argument: 

  1. The manner in which the fire is stolen. Hesiod writes that Prometheus stole the fire in a hollow fennel stalk — for Freud, this represents the penis. 
  2. The character of the act: a theft from the gods. Freud views gods as representative of the instinctual part of humanity, the id. 
  3. The punishment: an eagle comes along to eat out Prometheus’s liver on a daily basis. Birds are penis symbols, according to Freud; the eagle (penis symbol) eats at the liver (the container of emotions or passions), in a way that consoles humanity. 

Freud concludes that it is understandable that humanity would be frustrated by the constant suppression of the urge to extinguish the fire. However, Freud determines that other satisfaction is okay.

Today is all about the Pantheon of Gods. 

Here’s the order in which the gods come:

Aphrodite/Venus: born from the seafoam

Zeus/Jupiter: born from the titans

Posiedon/Neptune: born from the titans

Hades/Pluto: born from the titans; lived in underworld

Hera/Juno: born from the titans

Demeter/Ceres: born from the titans

Hestia/Vesta: born from the titans

Athena/Minerva: child of Zeus

Ares/Mars: child of Zeus

Artemis/Diana: child of Zeus

Apollo: child of Zeus

Hermes/Mercury: child of Zeus and a nymph

Dionysus/Bacchus: child of Zeus and Semele (a mortal)

Hephaestus/Vulcan: born from Hera

Note that there are 13 gods listed here: how do we get to 12 on Mt. Olympus? Generally, it was accepted that Dionysus and Hestia shared space on the mountain, or Hestia stepped aside to allow Dionysus to take her spot. 

The divinities living on Mt. Olympus were also known as ouranioi, or “heavenly ones.” Why do they live on a mountain (part of the earth) but exist in the “sky?”  

The Greeks believed that down below, where the humans and animals lived, people breathed normal air. However, if you went high enough up (say, by climbing Mt. Olympus), you would instead breathe aither or ether. Instead of wine, the divinities drank nectar; instead of food, the divinities ate ambrosia. The divinities also had ichor coursing through their veins instead of blood. All of this combined to make the Greek gods immortal and ageless forever. 

The goddess Hebe is the cupbearer of the gods.

The goddess Iris, also a rainbow goddess, is a messenger; she carries a caduceus like Hermes does. She’s mostly a messenger for Hera. 

How do we recognize the figures of Greek mythology in Greek art?

  1. Sometimes, there’s an actual name presented. Once you take elementary Greek, you can read what that name says.
  2. Look for the attributes of a divinity. For example, the eagle and the lightning bolt are attributes of Zeus.
  3. Pay attention to the context! If you can recognize the narrative being represented in the work of art, you can identify the divinities involved. 
  4. Sometimes, divinities are paired; their pairing suggests something of significance. If you can identify one member of the pair, you might be able to come up with the second.

Zeus: Zeus’s homeric hymn: Zeus is linked to Themis, indicating that he will be on the side of law and order. Zeus is referred to as the son of Kronos. He is once again referred to as the leader of the gods. A lot of Greek art depicts a symposium, with the gods sitting around and talking and debating and typically arguing. Zeus is typically known for cheating on Hera, and Hera is commonly jealous and fighting with Zeus. They would usually just sit around and talk about things. 

Hera: Hera is typically referred to as the “sister and wife” of Zeus. Associated with women and childbirth, commonly. 

Poseidon: He is typically represented by the trident in artwork. Poseidon is implored to be the guardian of horses and the savior of ships; also called the “Earth-shaker” and commonly blamed for earthquakes. 

Hestia: Hestia is the goddess of the hearth in the home. She’s also a goddess of chastity; known as very chaste. There are very few myths about Hestia. She appears in art as part of the Greek entourage, but there really isn’t much else to know about her. 

Ares: Ares is the god of war and battle. He is depicted in the Homeric hymns as a “savior of cities,” but typically in mythology, he represents the crazed/manic side of war. Typically, Athena is the defender of the city or of heroes. Ares is also known for his long-running relationship with Aphrodite. 

Athena: defensive goddess of war; also, wisdom. The owl is a symbol.

Aphrodite: goddess of love and sexuality. Usually depicted naked.

Hephaestus: Hephaestus is known as the god of crafts. He forges things and builds technology, known for his skill. He is the child of Hera alone, and is often depicted as lame.

Today’s focus: the lusts of Zeus.  

In Zeus’s lusts, he is typically depicted comically — it’s ironic that this grand deity becomes almost subhuman in his subjugation to his desires. 

There is also a combination of different tones in the stories of Zeus’s lust in Ovid’s Metamorphoses. He writes comically, but also maintains sympathy for the victims of Zeus’s lusts; however, in some stories, he’s more interested in how this affects Zeus. Oftentimes, the antagonist in these stories is not Zeus but Hera, depicted as a jealous wife who will sometimes take vengeance on her cheating husband. 

Zeus and Semele: 

Zeus and Semele are in a relationship, and Zeus impregnates Semele. This really pisses Hera off, and she swears that she will ruin the life of Semele. 

Hera convinces Semele to ask Zeus to come and visit her in bed as his true self on Olympus. He promises that he will do this, but then realizes that if he does so — goes with his thunder and lightning, etc. — Semele will definitely die. He becomes a simpering god, tries to choose his “weakest” lightning bolts, but it’s a hapless effort; Semele is killed in bed, and Semele’s child Dionysus is taken and sewn into the thigh of Zeus. 

Zeus and Ganymede: 

Zeus steals a young boy, takes him to Mt. Olympus, and turns him into his cupbearer. 

Hera has a few reasons to object: first, a boy has been brought onto Olympus. Second, her daughter with Zeus (Hebe) is already the cupbearer on Olympus.
